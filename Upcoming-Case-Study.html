About the project

Recent research shows that 2.4 million Australians do not know when their regular bills are due, leaving them vulnerable to late fees and financial stress. Broader financial pressure is also rising, with 28 percent of households reporting at least one cash‑flow problem in the past year. Unexpected bills are especially destabilising, with almost two thirds of households unable to comfortably pay a surprise $2,000 expense.

Upcoming was created to address this growing problem. It predicts the bills a customer is likely to receive in the next 30 days by analysing their historical bill patterns. By giving customers visibility of what is coming, the tool helps reduce financial stress and supports better planning.

Objective

The goal of Upcoming is to give customers a clear forecast of their upcoming expenses so they can manage their cash flow with confidence.

By helping customers avoid missed payments and unexpected shortfalls, the tool supports stronger financial habits, increases trust in the banking experience, and ultimately contributes to healthier deposit behaviour.

Challenges

#1 Slow back-end system

Based on historical data, any changes to a bill would not be reflected until the next payment occurred

#2 Two previous bill requirement

Forecasting required at least two previous bill payments to establish a pattern, delaying new bills showing for two bill cycles.

#3 Delayed actions

Bill payments take two days to process, meaning a bill may remain in a forecasted state even after the customer pays it

Challenging the challenges

Issue

Slow back-end

Solution

I designed functionality that allowed customers to edit or delete predicted bills directly.

This gave them immediate control over their forecast rather than waiting for the system to catch up.

Issue

Delayed forecasting

Solution

I designed the ability to add bills so customers could start forecasting expenses two bill cycles earlier.

I pushed the experience further by allowing pre-filled data based on any previous similar bills. This way, customers didn’t have to fill the form from scratch.

Issue

Delayed actions

Solution

Adding the ability to mark bills as paid meant customers could clear their forecasted bills quickly while their bill payment was being processed.

I pushed for swipe functionality to reduce friction and boost efficiency.

Prototype and testing

We ran two rounds of moderated testing with 12 participants in each round, giving us a total of 24 participants across the study.

The first round performed well, achieving an approval rating of 4.32 out of 5, but it also revealed a key issue: many customers struggled to understand how their payments influenced the bill forecast.

Based on this insight, we refined the experience by:

  • Updates included clearer copy
  • Adjustments to the interface to make it feel more like a read‑only forecast rather than a direct debit management tool
  • Improved visual cues to help customers interpret predicted bills.

The second round of testing showed significant improvement. Confusion around payment behaviour dropped noticeably, and the approval rating increased to 4.62/5 (7% increase).

These results validated that the refinements made the forecasting model feel more intuitive and trustworthy.

Design to development

Working with overseas development teams

With the development team working in a different time zone, most collaboration happened asynchronously. I set up a workflow where each completed user story triggered a desk check, helping us validate builds quickly. Clear, open communication ensured developers could surface questions easily and stay aligned with the intended experience.

Documentations

I documented every scenario, edge case and error state to give developers a complete reference. Using a shell‑based structure allowed rapid updates across all screens, keeping the file consistent and reducing rework.

Animation and design system contribution

Because animation was new within NAB’s design system, I utlised Google Material 3 animation tokens to define speeds and easing. I also built a Figma prototype using custom transitions to demonstrate the motion behaviour in context. I worked with both the design system team and gained the Chief Design Officer’s support ot push animations into NABs design ecosystem.
